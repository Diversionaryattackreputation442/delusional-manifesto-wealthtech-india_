# Product Spec: Council Mode

> *When AI agents debate your financial decisions so you don't have to*

## The Premise

Every big financial decision is haunted by the same fear: *"What if I'm wrong?"*

Should I buy a house or keep renting? Should I take this job offer? Should I put ₹10L in equity or wait for the dip? Should I prepay my home loan or invest the surplus?

Today, you either: (a) agonize alone, (b) ask friends who know less than you, (c) ask a financial advisor who has a commission bias, or (d) Google it and drown in contradictory advice.

Council Mode gives you something that has never existed before: **multiple expert AI perspectives debating your specific decision, using your actual financial data, in real-time.**

Not one opinion. A *council* of perspectives. Each with a clear stance, clear reasoning, and clear tradeoffs. You listen to the debate. You decide.

## How It Works

### Trigger
User asks a complex financial question:
> "Should I buy a 2BHK in Whitefield for ₹85L or continue renting at ₹28K/month?"

### The Council Assembles

The system spins up 3-4 specialized AI perspectives, each with a distinct analytical lens and access to the user's full financial data:

**The Wealth Maximizer** 🏛️
Optimizes purely for long-term net worth growth. Cold, mathematical, opportunity-cost obsessed.

**The Risk Guardian** 🛡️
Focused on financial resilience, downside protection, and "what could go wrong" scenarios.

**The Life Optimizer** 🌿
Considers quality of life, emotional wellbeing, life stage, and non-financial factors.

**The Tax Strategist** 📊
Sees every decision through the tax lens. What's the most tax-efficient path?

### The Debate

Each council member presents their position using the user's *actual* data:

---

**🏛️ The Wealth Maximizer:**
> "Don't buy. The math is clear.
>
> Rent: ₹28K/month = ₹3.36L/year.
> Buy: EMI at ₹85L (₹17L down + ₹68L loan at 8.5%) = ₹58,500/month = ₹7.02L/year. Plus maintenance ₹8K/month, property tax ₹12K/year, insurance ₹6K/year. Total annual cost of ownership: ₹8.3L.
>
> If you invest the ₹17L down payment + the monthly difference (₹30K+) in equity for 15 years, you'll have ₹1.8-2.2Cr. The apartment will be worth ₹1.4-1.6Cr (Whitefield CAGR ~6-7%). You're richer by ₹30-60L if you rent and invest.
>
> The only reason to buy is if you'll live there 15+ years AND you can't trust yourself to invest the surplus. Which, based on your investment history, is a real concern."

---

**🛡️ The Risk Guardian:**
> "The Wealth Maximizer is right on returns. But they're missing three risks:
>
> 1. Rental inflation: ₹28K/month today is ₹56K/month in 10 years at Bangalore's 7-8% rental inflation. Your EMI stays flat. By year 8, buying is cheaper in cash flow terms.
>
> 2. Career concentration risk: You work in tech. Your income, your investments (heavy equity), and your lifestyle are all tech-correlated. If tech has a multi-year downturn, your income drops, your portfolio drops, AND you still need to pay rent. A paid-off house is uncorrelated insurance.
>
> 3. Your emergency fund is only 3.8 months. If you buy, it drops to 1.2 months after the down payment. That's dangerously low. If you buy, you MUST rebuild to 6 months before you do anything else.
>
> My recommendation: Buy, but not now. Spend 8 months building your emergency fund to 8 months (₹6.5L). Then buy with a stronger safety net."

---

**🌿 The Life Optimizer:**
> "Neither of them asked the question that matters: Do you want to own a home?
>
> You've mentioned buying a home in 4 conversations this year. You've been browsing property listings for 3 months. Your partner has been sharing Whitefield apartment links. This isn't purely a financial decision.
>
> Owning a home provides stability, identity, and emotional security that doesn't show up in a spreadsheet. If this apartment lets you settle down, focus on your career without rental anxiety, and build a life — the ₹30-60L 'opportunity cost' might be the best investment you never see a return on.
>
> That said: don't let emotion override safety. The Risk Guardian's point about the emergency fund is critical. Don't buy until you have 6 months of expenses as backup."

---

**📊 The Tax Strategist:**
> "A few things no one has mentioned:
>
> 1. Home loan interest deduction: Under Section 24, you can deduct up to ₹2L/year in interest. At your tax bracket (30%), that's ₹60,000/year in tax savings. The Wealth Maximizer's comparison didn't account for this.
>
> 2. Principal repayment under Section 80C: Up to ₹1.5L/year. But you're already maxing 80C through ELSS and PPF. So this has zero incremental benefit for you.
>
> 3. Capital gains: If you rent and invest, your equity gains will be taxed at 10% (LTCG above ₹1L). The property appreciation is taxed only on sale, with indexation benefits. Tax-adjusted returns narrow the gap.
>
> 4. Stamp duty and registration: ₹5-6L in Karnataka. This is a sunk cost that needs 2-3 years just to recover. Don't buy unless you're staying 5+ years.
>
> Net tax impact: Buying saves you approximately ₹50-60K/year in taxes. This improves the buy case but doesn't flip the overall math."

---

### The Synthesis

After the debate, the system synthesizes:

> **Council Verdict: Conditional Buy**
>
> The council leans toward buying, but with conditions:
>
> 1. First: Build emergency fund to 6 months (₹6.5L). Timeline: ~8 months at your current savings rate.
> 2. Then: Buy the Whitefield 2BHK with ₹17L down payment.
> 3. Restructure: Pause ELSS SIP (you'll get 80C from home loan principal). Redirect to emergency fund replenishment.
> 4. Don't overstretch: Your EMI-to-income ratio will be 39%. Comfortable, but leaves little room for lifestyle inflation. Lock your discretionary budget for 12 months.
>
> The Wealth Maximizer dissents: renting and investing is still financially optimal. But the council recognizes that financial optimization isn't the only variable that matters.
>
> **Want me to create the 8-month preparation plan?**

## Why This Works

### Decision Quality
Humans are terrible at multi-perspective analysis. We anchor on one perspective (usually the most emotionally resonant) and ignore the others. Council Mode forces exposure to all perspectives.

### Confidence
The user doesn't just get an answer. They get a *debate*. They see the strongest arguments on every side. When they decide, they decide with confidence — not because they ignored the counterarguments, but because they weighed them.

### Trust
By showing disagreement, Council Mode earns trust. A system that always says "yes, great idea!" feels like a sycophant. A system that says "here are three reasons to do it and two reasons not to" feels like a genuine advisor.

### Engagement
The debate format is inherently engaging. People read all four perspectives because each one reveals something new about their own situation. It's not just informative — it's compelling.

## Trigger Criteria

Council Mode activates for decisions that are:
- **Irreversible or high-stakes**: Home purchase, job change, large investment, insurance selection
- **Multi-dimensional**: Involves tradeoffs across financial, lifestyle, and risk dimensions
- **Ambiguous**: No objectively "right" answer — depends on values and priorities

Council Mode does NOT activate for:
- Simple questions: "What's the best liquid fund?" → Direct answer
- Urgent actions: "I need emergency cash now" → Ghost Mode emergency protocol
- Routine decisions: "Should I continue my SIP?" → Yes, with brief rationale

## The Council Members Expand

The initial four perspectives (Wealth Maximizer, Risk Guardian, Life Optimizer, Tax Strategist) can be extended based on the decision domain:

- **Career decisions**: Add "The Career Strategist" (income trajectory, industry outlook)
- **Insurance decisions**: Add "The Actuary" (probability-based risk analysis)
- **Real estate decisions**: Add "The Market Analyst" (local real estate trends, rental yield analysis)
- **Business decisions**: Add "The Entrepreneur" (opportunity cost, startup economics)

The council adapts to the question.
